  🏦  SECTOR SPOTLIGHT: BANKING — 31 DOWN, 2 TO GO, ₦7 TRILLION DOING ABSOLUTELY NOTHING.

₦7,000,000,000,000.

That is how much Nigeria's banks parked at the CBN this week. Idle. Earning a modest overnight rate. Financing nothing. Not a road, not a factory, not a small business, not your cousin's catering startup. Just sitting there, blinking.

THE WEEK IN THREE SENTENCES.

Brent crude crossed $101 intraday on Wednesday — the first triple-digit oil print since the war began — before settling at $99.80 by Friday. Nigeria's banks completed the most significant recapitalisation exercise in twenty years, with 31 of 33 banks meeting the CBN's new capital requirements — and are now sitting on ₦7 trillion in excess liquidity they don't know how to deploy. Meanwhile, the 2026 federal budget still has not passed, the Senate adjourned to March 31, and Nigeria is staring at a potential ₦30 trillion oil windfall with no legal framework yet to receive it.

The number to watch above all others: the February 2026 inflation print, due this week from the NBS. Ten months of consecutive decline has been the backbone of every bullish argument about Nigeria — CBN rate cut, Naira stability, NGX rally, all of it. If February comes in higher than January's 15.10%, none of those dominoes are as stable as they look. That single data point is the week's entire storyline. Everything else is context.

🏛️  THE MAIN STORY

The Budget That Isn't — and Why It's the Most Expensive Silence in Abuja Right Now.

Here is a number nobody is saying loudly enough: Nigeria is 103 days into the 2026 fiscal year without a passed budget. The ₦58.18 trillion spending plan — which includes ₦26 trillion in capital expenditure for roads, power infrastructure, education, and health — has been sitting with the National Assembly since December. The Senate adjourned this week and will next sit on March 31. That is the same date as the CBN bank recapitalisation deadline. Somebody in the scheduling office has a sense of drama.

What this actually costs: every week the budget remains unpassed, the capital release mechanism is frozen. Ministries are rationing. Contractors are waiting. The multiplier effect of government spending — which economists estimate at 1.3–1.6x in Nigeria's context — is not running. Now layer the oil windfall on top: the NESG this week published a policy brief titled "Boom, Not Gloom" mapping a potential ₦30.2 trillion in extra oil revenue if the Hormuz disruption persists through Q2. That is transformational money. But without a passed budget, the government has no legal framework to save it, deploy it, or protect it from being quietly absorbed into recurrent expenditure. The windfall is coming. The box to put it in is under construction.

Finance Minister Wale Edun explicitly ruled out petrol price controls this week — holding the deregulation line despite ₦1,200/litre petrol and very loud political pressure. That deserves credit. But Edun is making the right macroeconomic argument to a population experiencing the wrong kitchen-table reality. The theory is sound. The bus fare from Surulere to the Island is ₦800 now. Both things are true.

"The windfall is real. The machinery to receive it doesn't legally exist yet. Nigeria is standing in front of the biggest payday of this decade, and the Senate adjourned."

💵  NAIRA WAHALA

Something quietly remarkable happened this week: Brent touched $101 and the Naira moved by half a percent. Last year, $80 oil with a Hormuz rumour would have moved the parallel rate by 15%. The parallel market spread is now 0.2–1.3% — the tightest since the 2023 reforms. The Dangote Refinery at 40 million litres per day is reducing the forex Nigeria would otherwise need to import refined products. Export dollar inflows — running 30–60 days behind the Brent price — are accumulating in the CBN's reserves, which are holding at $50.45 billion. The Naira is not strong because everything is fine. The Naira is stable because the structural architecture around it is finally working — for the first time in years.

📈  THE FLOOR

The NGX is up 47.8% year-to-date. For context, the S&P 500 is up roughly 3% in the same period. Madam Market is not walking — she is sprinting, and she is doing it in a freshly recapitalised banking sector. Thirty-one banks crossed the CBN's new capital threshold this week. The two remaining are the only suspense left in an exercise that began in March 2024. One will raise capital at the wire. One may be acquired. Either way, Nigeria exits this exercise with fewer but stronger institutions — positioned to finance a windfall cycle that nobody could have predicted when the exercise was designed.

Which brings us back to the ₦7 trillion. Banks are liquid. Banks are also afraid. The Standing Deposit Facility — the CBN window where banks park excess cash overnight — is the banking sector's version of sleeping with money under the mattress. In a 26.5% interest rate environment, the risk-adjusted return on corporate lending doesn't beat the zero-effort SDF rate by enough to justify the credit risk. The CBN's rate cut was supposed to change this calculus. Watch whether the SDF balance falls below ₦5 trillion by end of Q2. That is the real leading indicator of whether Nigeria's recovery has reached the real economy — not the NGX.

🧂  Seplat Energy announced 17 new wells and $440 million in capex for 2026 — the most aggressive indigenous upstream program in years, timed perfectly into $100 oil. The market may not have fully priced this in yet.

⚖️  THE VERDICT

VILLAIN OF THE WEEK

The Senate. Not because they are incompetent — but because the cost of this adjournment is specific and measurable. Every week without a passed budget is ₦500 billion in capital expenditure timing delayed. At a 1.4x multiplier, that's ₦700 billion in economic activity not being generated per week of delay. The Senators go home. The contractors wait. The roads don't get built. The ₦30 trillion windfall arrives into an empty room.

HERO OF THE WEEK

CBN Governor Olayemi Cardoso. The bank recapitalisation exercise he designed, pressured, and saw through to 31/33 completion is arguably the most consequential institutional achievement in Nigerian banking since Soludo's 2005 consolidation. He didn't announce it loudly. He didn't need to. The balance sheets speak for themselves.
