ACT I — THE PHYSICS OF STEEZE

Escape Velocity vs. Lekki Landlord Rent

Let’s deconstruct corporate finance, physics, and the art of the macro-grift. On June 12, SpaceX listed on the NASDAQ under the ticker SPCX at a $1.77 Trillion valuation, raising $75 Billion in a single afternoon. If you build reusable rockets that go to Mars, global capital hands you equity. Equity is permanent, non-debt capital. Elon Musk does not promise investors a 26.5% interest coupon. He promises them Mars. If the rocket explodes, he owes them nothing. Pure steeze, no cap. [1]

Meanwhile, in Abuja, the Central Bank of Nigeria is popping champagne because our gross external reserves crossed the $50.35 Billion milestone. It is a beautiful spreadsheet number, the highest in 17 years. But there is a minor balance sheet detail that our politicians conveniently skip during their Democracy Day speeches: this is not saved oil revenue, and it is not foreign direct investment. It is rented cash.

To keep $4.8 Billion in hot carry-trade money sitting in our vault to defend the Naira, CBN Governor Olayemi Cardoso has to pay foreign portfolio managers yields of up to 21% on OMO bills (underwritten by a 26.5% central bank policy rate). That is the financial equivalent of renting a crowd for a political rally—just like those Amuneke skits on YouTube where they hire boys to carry campaign banners and chant name wey they no know. You pay them ₦10,000 to hold your posters and shout your name so the TV cameras show you are popular. The moment the broadcast ends, the crowd goes home.

₦156.7 Million per hour. Around the clock. Every hour of the year.

Nominally, the numbers mirror each other: Nigeria pays **₦1.37 Trillion** in annual rent; SpaceX is valued at **$1.77 Trillion**. But one is in Naira, and it is a *yearly expense* paid to Manhattan hedge funds. The other is in Dollars, and it is *permanent equity value* created by building reusable space rockets.

If Nigeria wanted to buy SpaceX using the money it spends *just on the annual interest to rent its reserves*, it would take us **1,756 years** of interest payments (at NAFEM ₦1,362/$) to match SpaceX’s equity value. We are running a premium rental service for foreign funds while choking domestic credit. 😐

ACT II — THE STREET

Step out of Aso Rock. Street na ₦1,400.

Broad Street, Lagos. The sun is aggressive, reflecting off the glass of bank headquarters where executive directors are sipping coffee. Downstairs on the asphalt, Alhaji sits on a plastic chair under a fading umbrella, fanning himself with green USD bills. He has more currency liquidity in his pockets than half the microfinance banks in the state.

“Alhaji, NAFEM dey ₦1,362 o. Spread done collapse!”

Alhaji doesn’t even blink. He flips a crisp $100 note. “My friend. NAFEM na for television. Broad Street parallel na ₦1,395. Summer travel season. Everybody want dollar. You buy at ₦1,400 or you call Cardoso direct. Broad Street does not buy vibes. We buy dollar.” [2]

The official window is a beautiful aquarium. It has clean water, controlled feeding, and pretty fish. But the street is the open ocean. If you are an actual business importing raw materials, you don’t live in the aquarium. You swim with the sharks in Alhaji’s parallel market. And the sharks charge ₦1,400.

🎓 THE ALPHA CLASSROOM — STREET-LEVEL EDUCATION

Every week, we deconstruct a complex financial concept that the suit-and-tie crowd uses to confuse you. Today’s lesson: Permanent Capital (Equity) vs. Rented Capital (Carry Debt).

🚀 SpaceX: Permanent Capital
Investors buy Equity (shares). The money stays in SpaceX’s bank account forever to build rockets. If the rocket explodes, Elon Musk doesn’t owe them a kobo in interest. They share the long-term risk.
  
💵 Cardoso: Rented Capital
Hedge funds bring dollars to buy OMO Bills at ~21% interest. The dollars sit in our vault, but we must return them in 180 days plus interest. This money has running shoes; it is not ours.

The Street Translation:

Equity (Permanent Capital) is like marrying a partner and building a home together. If storm blows the roof, you both fix it. Nobody is charging you rent. You are in it together for the long haul.

Carry Trade (Rented Capital) is like renting a three-story duplex in Lekki Phase 1 for ₦100 Million a year just to show your ex that you have arrived. You don’t own the house. The moment you cannot pay the annual rent, the landlord throws your box of agbada onto the express. Nigeria is currently in the Lekki duplex, and Wall Street is the landlord collecting ₦156.7 Million an hour in rent. Steeze is high, but the rent is killing the business.

ACT III — THE YIELD TRAP & ECONOMIC GRAVITY

Choking the code to pay the rent.

Consider the intellectual contradiction of our current economic policy. The President stands at the Democracy Day podium and urges youth to “stay on the ship and code here.” Meanwhile, the Central Bank sets the policy rate at 26.5%. [3]

If the government is paying up to 21% risk-free yield to Manhattan hedge funds to hold dollars in our vault, no local bank is going to lend to a local tech startup, agricultural processor, or manufacturer at less than 32-35%. The risk-free rate acts as economic gravity. It sucks all liquid capital out of productive business and traps it in government debt. We are literally starving the youth of the capital they need to “build here” just to pay rent to Wall Street.


 “SpaceX sells equity to escape Earth’s gravity. Nigeria pays ₦3.76 Billion daily in interest to rent the financial gravity that keeps our economy pinned to the floor.”

And then comes the political schedule. The new Electoral Act 2026 permits a presidential candidate to spend ₦10 Billion legally on campaigns. When you multiply that by 36 governors, 109 senators, and 360 house members, you get a massive liquidity bomb scheduled for Q4 2026. And that is just the legal, audited money. Just look at the recent headlines of party delegates flying to Kigali for a “capacity building workshop on democratic values,” only to return with brand new leather agbadas and a sudden, passionate belief in “continuity.” [4] The street knows what capacity was actually built there. The un-audited campaign logistics—bullion vans are already doing warmups at party headquarters, and customized campaign umbrellas are being printed with deep cash compartments sewn into the lining—will flood the streets, bid up Alhaji’s parallel rate, and pressure food prices. Fear who no fear Nigerian politicians.

Here is the delicious irony: the politicians about to flood the economy with campaign Naira are the same people who rely on Cardoso’s rented stability to keep their dollar assets safe. They are standing on the very ground they are preparing to burn. The carry trade defends the currency, but politics is on a very fixed, very well-funded calendar. When campaign money hits the street, steeze go bend.

💡 WHAT SMART MONEY IS DOING RIGHT NOW
Three actionable calls from a macro desk that prefers arithmetic over speech-writing.

1. Settle Q3/Q4 dollar commitments immediately.

Cardoso’s rented reserves are at their peak ($50.35B), and NAFEM is holding at ₦1,362. This is as good as the anchor gets. If you have dollar obligations for raw materials or overseas school fees, buy your dollars now. Do not wait for Q4 campaign liquidity to pressure Alhaji’s parallel rate past ₦1,450. Lock your steeze now.

2. Harvest the OMO yields but set a calendar alarm.

Earning ~21% on government papers is an extraordinary deal, but it is a crisis-defense rate. It is a sprint, not a pension. Keep your cash in short-dated papers (90 to 180 days) so you can pivot when the political campaign liquidity begins to dilute the CBN’s efforts. Doings are sweet, but caution is key.

3. Rotate out of rate-sensitive sectors into defensive pricing power.

The NGX All-Share holds at 244,697.62, but local equity is squeezed by rate gravity. Rotate out of banking stocks—which will face margin compression once this high-rate window closes—and buy consumer defensive names (telcos, food staples) that can pass structural inflation directly to the street.
