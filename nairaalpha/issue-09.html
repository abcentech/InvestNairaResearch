NIGERIA'S OIL YEAR — WRITTEN BY PEOPLE WHO AREN'T NIGERIAN

On Thursday, Brent touched $119.85. It was the kind of number that arrives with fanfare: OPEC producers smiled, Nigerian budget planners quietly revised their spreadsheets upward, and somewhere in Abuja a civil servant had his most productive afternoon of the year. Then Trump held a press conference. Said the war might be wrapping up in "two or three weeks." By the next morning, Brent was $101. The spreadsheet went back to normal. Nigeria had been a spectator at both ends of the ride.

This is the structural problem that NNPC CEO Bashir Ojulari flew to London this week to pitch around. His year-one numbers are genuinely impressive — production up from 250,000 to 355,000 barrels per day, the $20 billion Bonga Southwest FID finally signed after a decade of 'almost,' a $2.5 billion Afreximbank facility closed, and an NNPC-Dangote strategic alliance formalised. If NNPC were a private company and Ojulari had delivered this in any other sector, there would be a Bloomberg profile and a dinner at the Intercontinental. But the NYSE/LSE dual-listing pitch rests on an oil price that moved $18 in two days because of a press conference. That is not a foundation for a listing. That is a weather forecast.

Downstream, the week proved exactly why the IPO story will be hard to sell before the basics are fixed. Dangote moved its gantry price four times in two weeks. Pump prices in Lagos didn't flinch. The gap between refinery gate and forecourt is not a logistics mystery — it is margin captured at every checkpoint between Lekki and your dashboard. Oga Macro deregulated the downstream sector. The downstream sector deregulated itself right back.
