THE EARNINGS CAME. THEY WERE REAL. THE RALLY WAS NOT MADE OF AIR.

The most dangerous thing about a bull market is that it can run on hope before it runs on numbers. When the NGX had returned 30% in Q1, the sceptics had a reasonable position: the rally was speculative, inflation-driven, disconnected from underlying corporate performance. Na packaging dem dey package. Q1 earnings delivered the definitive answer this week. The rally was not made of air. It was made of ₦1 trillion in Zenith Bank quarterly revenue — the first time a Nigerian bank has ever crossed that threshold — ₦178 billion in Presco profit, a Okomu Oil sector printing quarterly numbers that embarrass the sceptics, and an NGX Group that nearly doubled its own PAT because the exchange's transaction fee income jumped 189% on the back of the same rally everyone said was not real.

Zenith Bank's numbers deserve their own paragraph. Gross revenue: ₦1.01 trillion. Pre-tax profit: ₦360.9 billion. Fee and commission income: ₦81 billion — up 44.5% — which is the digital banking story, the transaction volume story, the fact that more Nigerians are moving more money through formal channels than at any point before. The stock is up 108% year-to-date. If you bought ₦1,000,000 of Zenith shares on January 2 and did nothing until today, you have ₦2,080,000. Your cousin who said sell: he still hasn't called. Emeka is still waiting for Christmas.

The agricultural story is quieter but equally important. Presco and Okomu are not exciting companies in the way that fintech is exciting. They are palm oil companies in Delta and Edo state, growing food inputs that Nigeria cannot easily import because the naira made importing expensive, and selling at margins that expand when the naira stabilises and global cooking oil prices are elevated. Person wey hold Presco since January go beg to differ with anybody wey call agric sector "boring."

$116 BRENT. IRAN SAID NO. $150 IS NOW A SCENARIO.

Iran's supreme leader Khamenei this week did what supreme leaders do when they want absolute clarity: he said no. No deal without nuclear concessions. Iran will not relinquish nuclear or missile capabilities. Iran retains control of the Strait of Hormuz. Trump simultaneously faces a 60-day War Powers Act deadline — under the 1973 law, troops must be withdrawn unless Congress authorises deployment, which it has not done. Brent hit $116 on Thursday. The IEA called this an "unprecedented supply shock." Multiple bank analysts now have $150 as a scenario. Na serious wahala. The kind wey nobody plan for for the 2026 budget meeting.

Nigeria's budget was benchmarked at $75 per barrel. At $116, every barrel earns $41 above benchmark. At 1.5 million barrels per day, the daily windfall calculation produces a very large naira figure. This explains why the Naira official/parallel spread is ₦2 — oil inflows are real, they are going into the system, they are supporting the currency. On the other side: pump prices in Lagos crossed ₦1,400 per litre this week. The upstream and downstream economies of Nigeria have officially stopped having the same conversation about oil.

The Seplat footnote: workers called off an indefinite strike after PENGASSAN reached agreement on pay rises. Production resumed. The timing is notable — Seplat's stock is near all-time highs, oil is at $116, and the strike lasted just long enough to make a point about how the oil windfall gets shared internally. Na fair point, honestly. The people extracting the oil have opinions too.
