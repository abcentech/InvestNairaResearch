NGX CROSSED $100 BILLION. YOUR UNCLE WHO SAID SELL IN JANUARY IS VERY QUIET.

The NGX All-Share Index closed Wednesday at 219,586 points. Year-to-date: +41.11%. Market capitalisation in naira: ₦141.4 trillion. Market capitalisation in US dollars: $105 billion. The last time the Nigerian stock exchange was worth $100 billion in dollar terms was 2008, during the retail margin-lending bubble that ended in tears, investigations, and a very uncomfortable few years for everyone involved. This time na different tin. That is not wishful thinking — it is the architecture.

In 2008, the rally was built on retail investors borrowing to buy bank stocks in a circular loop that looked like growth until it looked like a crisis. In 2026, the rally is built on: the completion of banking recapitalisation, the reinstatement of Nigeria to the FTSE Russell Frontier Index (meaning global passive money has been flowing in since 2025), Pension Fund Administrator inflows running at ₦2.18 trillion projected for the full year, and an actual earnings recovery across banking, industrials, and consumer goods. The 26 companies now worth ₦1 trillion or more include Guinness, Presco, and Okomu Oil Palm — sectors that did not exist in the 2008 cohort. The market is wider than it was. E don grow properly.

The Banking Index is up +54% year-to-date. The Premium Board: +61.83%. The Pension Index: +58.82%. The NGX Oil & Gas Index is up +91.63% and at this point should probably apologise to every other sector for making them look underperforming. Seplat Energy is the kind of stock that makes investors who held through 2023 feel like geniuses, and investors who sold in 2023 feel like they need a long walk and some perspective.

The risk is the one Naira Alpha has been flagging: Q1 2026 earnings season begins this week. Banks are reporting. If any earnings disappoint — and after a +54% sector YTD run, "meeting expectations" might not be enough — the profit-taking that we predicted (and got wrong on timing) will happen. Consolidation in the 212,000–218,000 range is the analyst base case. We call it: the market takes a deep breath before deciding whether to sprint again.

OIL IS BACK ABOVE $100 AND YOUR PUMP PRICE IS REVIEWING THE SITUATION.

The week's oil story is genuinely cinematic. On Thursday, Trump posted on Truth Social that he had ordered the US Navy to "shoot and kill" any vessels laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz. He also boarded an Iranian supertanker in the Indian Ocean. Iran responded by saying it had no interest in negotiating while the blockade remained in effect. Talks were extended "indefinitely." Brent, observing all of this from the sidelines, climbed above $106. The EIA confirmed what traders already knew: Brent backwardation — where spot prices exceed futures prices — hit a $25/barrel premium, meaning the market is physically desperate for oil right now, not in three months. That kind of signal last happened in 2022 when Russia invaded Ukraine. Na serious matter be this.

For Nigeria, the arithmetic is interesting in both directions. The 2026 budget was benchmarked at $75/bbl. At $106, every barrel of Nigerian crude earns roughly $31 more than budgeted. Production is currently around 1.5–1.8 million barrels per day. Do that multiplication at your leisure, but the answer starts with "₦" and has a lot of digits. On the other hand: Dangote Refinery sources crude in dollars. When Brent goes up, the refinery's input cost goes up, gantry prices follow eventually, and pump prices — in the inexplicable physics of Nigerian downstream economics — can go up before the gantry does and come down after it never quite does. This is not a new observation. It is a tradition at this point.

The ceasefire that brought oil to $90 last week is, at the time of writing, technically still in effect and also completely dysfunctional. The US and Iran have not met. Pakistan is trying to mediate. Trump is posting on Truth Social. The Strait remains closed. Brent is pricing all of this simultaneously and the result is $104 at the close of Thursday trading, with weekend geopolitical risk premium added on top. Nigeria is, as usual, along for the ride. We earn above our budget when it spikes. We pay above our means when it flows through to fuel. We dey manage.
