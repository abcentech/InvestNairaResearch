THE GREAT ROTATION: BANKS HAD THEIR MOMENT. THE MARKET IS MOVING ON.

This is the most important market structure story of May 2026. For four months, Nigerian banking stocks carried the NGX rally. The recapitalisation thesis played out perfectly: 33 banks raised ₦4.65 trillion, Q1 earnings confirmed the capital was real, and the NGXBNK index returned over 50% year-to-date. Then Q1 earnings season ended, the recapitalisation premium was fully priced, and institutional money asked the question it always eventually asks: what is the next trade?

Wednesday's session gave the answer: telecoms. MTN Nigeria gained ₦15.00 on high-conviction institutional volume — 5.7 million shares generating ₦4.7 billion in value, meaning large positions built at premium prices. Airtel Africa similarly bid. BUA Cement and Unilever joined. Meanwhile banking stocks saw profit-taking: GTCO -₦4.45, Zenith -₦4.00, First HoldCo -7.80% with 575 million shares changing hands for ₦44.4 billion. That is the largest single-session bank position liquidation of 2026. E no be problem. Na portfolio rebalancing.

The logic is straightforward. Banks were cheap in January because they needed capital. They raised it. That thesis is complete. Telecoms are now being repriced on a different thesis: inflation resistance. Airtel and MTN generate dollar-denominated revenues, have pricing power (Nigeria just approved telecom tariff increases), and their subscriber bases grow regardless of macroeconomic conditions. When inflation is 15.95% and heading higher, you want companies that can pass through price increases. Telecom companies can. Banks — it is more complicated.

APRIL INFLATION: 15.95%. BRENT: $120. THE CBN'S RATE-CUT WINDOW: CLOSING.

The Coronation Inflation Outlook this week confirmed what everyone who has been to a market in Lagos already knew: April 2026 inflation is projected at 15.95% — the second consecutive monthly rise since the 11-month disinflation streak ended in March. The mechanism is straightforward and depressingly familiar: Brent averaged $127.45 per barrel in April. That pushed PMS prices to an average of ₦1,322.50 — a 9.44% single-month increase in fuel costs. Transport costs feed food costs. Food costs feed the CPI. Na so the chain go.

The CBN has been cautiously cutting rates — the February cut of 50 basis points was described as "measured easing." At 15.95% inflation and Brent at $120, the next MPC meeting faces a harder choice. Cut further and risk embedding inflation above 16%. Hold and risk slowing credit growth and business investment. The interest rate tool works on demand-side inflation. When inflation is being driven by oil prices set in the Persian Gulf by parties Nigeria does not control, the interest rate is somewhat less useful. But it is the tool available and Cardoso will use it carefully.

Dangote Refinery ran at 99.1% capacity this week, producing 53.6 million litres per day against Nigeria's 51.1 million litre daily consumption. The refinery is now producing more petrol than Nigeria uses. Supply security: achieved. Price relief: still pending the downstream transmission mechanism, which has 10 weeks of documented evidence showing it does not transmit downward unless forced. With Brent at $120, the gantry is going up not down. The supply security is real. The affordability story is still being written. Two different chapters. Same book.
