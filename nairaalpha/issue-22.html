ROBBERY I — THE PHANTOM IPO

They Tried to Sell You a Movie That Hasn’t Started Shooting

In Lagos, financial opportunity doesn’t knock; it breathes heavily into your WhatsApp at 2:00 PM, bypassing both the spam filter and your common sense. The pitch is simple: Dangote Refinery pre-IPO shares. “Ground floor, my brother. Limited allocations. Just send the Naira to this custody account.” In Nigeria, a pre-IPO syndicate is often just three guys in Lekki with a Canva account, a shared workspace, and a collective dream of a private jet. Your thumb hovers over the send button. Don’t.

The SEC has issued a cease-and-desist. Eager brokers were packaging a “purported” public offering of Dangote Refinery shares. No application exists. The refinery is real; the petrol is real; but the pre-IPO prospectus was a Lagos masterpiece of creative writing in a PDF attachment. Capital market operators are selling boarding passes to a flight that hasn’t even been cleared by air traffic control.

While the SEC protects retail FOMO, Dangote bypasses local credit gravity entirely. Bypassing 26.5% MPR and 32% local bank rates, he walked straight to international institutional investors to raise $1 Billion in private offshore debt (approx. ₦1.37 Trillion), backed by Standard Bank. Why pay 32% interest to a Lagos bank manager who treats you like a debtor when you can borrow in single-digit USD from London? The billionaire exits via the offshore lounge; the local merchant stays behind to argue with the branch manager.

ROBBERY II — THE SOVEREIGN CROWDING OUT

Your Bank Sent Your Money to Orbit. Business Class.

Balogun Market operates on a simple law of economic thermodynamics: money flows to where it is warmest. Right now, that warmth is coming from Abuja.

If a bank manager has ₦10,000 to lend, and the Central Bank is offering 21.9% risk-free to sit on OMO bills, who gets the money? Is it the tailor buying a sewing machine, or is it the sovereign debt office that doesn’t need to collateralize its mother’s house?

The credit numbers are stark. Credit to the government stands at ₦40.38 Trillion. Meanwhile, private sector credit has shrunk by 14% year-to-date to ₦81.04 Trillion. The IMF warns debt servicing will consume over 50% of federal revenues in 2026. Lenders look away from the street to serve Abuja first.

🚨 WHERE THE BANKING SYSTEM’S MONEY ACTUALLY WENT
To Abuja (21.9% risk-free OMO yield): ₦40.38T — Stack height 4,441 km above earth
To the Private Sector (32% lending rate): ₦81.04T — YTD: −14%

Balogun math. Stack ₦40.38 Trillion in ₦1,000 notes (0.11mm thick). That tower rises 4,441.8 kilometres. Deep into the Low Earth Orbit satellite belt where Starlink sleeps. Your deposits are orbiting the earth next to Starlink satellites, collecting risk-free yields, while private sector credit has shrunk by 14% year-to-date. Your bank didn’t reject your business loan because they don’t like you; they rejected it because Abuja pays better rent and doesn’t run away with the generator.

💡 Macro footnote: The orbit is getting crowded. In June 2026, a projected maturity wave of ₦10.90 Trillion hit the system (including ₦7.77 Trillion maturing OMO). To lock this naira away from currency speculation, CBN mopped up ₦6.88 Trillion in two weeks at yields up to 21.9%. The banker’s business model is now simple: take your deposits, buy OMO, buy a new SUV, repeat. It’s risk-free, sweat-free, and street-free.

ROBBERY III — THE SPONGE JOB & THE STREET

₦125 Cut. ₦40 Savings. The Sponge Got Fed.

In economics, they call it asymmetric price transmission — the phenomenon where prices shoot up like a rocket when costs rise, but drift down like a feather when costs fall. In Lagos, we call it the Downstream Sponge.

Dangote Refinery sliced its ex-depot price by ₦125 per litre in a fortnight. A triumph of local industrialization! But by the time that fuel traveled from the Lekki Free Trade Zone to your local pump, the price cut had shrunk to ₦40. Where did the other ₦85 go?

It was swallowed by the downstream ecosystem: the marketers, the NARTO haulage unions, the local government tax collectors who appear from the gutter whenever money changes hands, and the retailers who suddenly discovered their storage tanks had “ulcerous logistics costs.”

📱 T+1 exit cycle: POS operators face registration pressure from CAC/EFCC. ATMs are dry; cash is being sterilized into OMOs, so operators tax the street.

And what are we doing with this expensive fuel? We are running generators at 2:00 AM to watch the World Cup. In the streets of Lagos, the GOAT debate is a matter of national security: Messi’s hat-trick versus Ronaldo’s tap-in.

Let’s look at the balance sheet. At NAFEM rates (₦1,368/$), Cristiano Ronaldo earns ₦273.6 Billion per year (₦749 Million per day). You, on the other hand, are burning ₦1,024 of petrol per match just to run your 2.5kVA generator and scream at the screen. The GOAT debate is free; the fuel is not.

In fact, watching the remaining 32 matches of this tournament on generator power will cost you about ₦31,000 in fuel. That represents nearly half (44% to 47%) of Nigeria’s official monthly minimum wage of ₦70,000. The government increased the minimum wage by ₦40,000 so that workers can afford to buy fuel to watch Messi’s penalty misses. That is not an inflation spiral; it is an entertainment tax.
