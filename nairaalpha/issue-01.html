🏛️  OGA AT THE TOP

Politics, policy, and the people who decide your wallet's fate

THE GDP: WHEN THE SCOREBOARD IS REAL BUT THE CROWD ISN'T CELEBRATING

Let us start with legitimately good news. Nigeria's full-year 2025 real GDP growth came in at 3.89% — the fastest pace since 2022, up from 3.38% in 2024. The NBS confirmed Q4 2025 alone hit 4.07% — one of the rare times in the past decade that a single quarter cleared 4%. The CBN projects 4.49% growth in 2026. Finance Minister Wale Edun is happy. The IMF is cautiously nodding. The man at Berger Bridge filling station is not nodding, but that's a different conversation.

🧂  ADD SALT: GDP numbers measure the size of the economy, not the comfort of your household. A 3.89% expansion is real — it means more economic activity, more investment, more production. But when cumulative inflation from 2023–2025 has eaten 40%+ of real purchasing power for many Nigerians, GDP growth is the scoreboard, not the feeling on the pitch.

THE OPL 245 DEAL: THE SLEEPER HEADLINE OF THE DECADE

While every financial journalist was watching oil charts, President Tinubu resolved one of the longest-running oil corruption scandals in Nigerian history. The OPL 245 offshore deepwater licence — the block that spent a decade in courts across four continents (Netherlands, Italy, Nigeria, UK, USA), destroying reputations and producing literally zero barrels of oil — is finally unlocked. Eni CEO Claudio Descalzi flew into Abuja. The block is split into four new deepwater assets. Development can begin.

For scale: OPL 245 sits in the same deepwater neighborhood as some of Nigeria's most productive offshore fields — the kind that produce hundreds of thousands of barrels per day each. At $91 oil, every barrel this block eventually produces is worth nearly double what was priced when many of the original legal battles were filed. The timing of this resolution — announced the same week Brent hit $91 — is either brilliant strategy or extraordinary coincidence. Either way: Oga Macro scores a 9.5 on this one.

"The OPL 245 resolution is the sleeper story of this entire week. Every journalist was watching the oil price. Tinubu was watching the oil block. In this specific instance: Oga Macro played chess."

THE CBN'S BOLD (OR RECKLESS, DEPENDING ON NEXT MONTH'S DATA) RATE CUT

The Central Bank cut the Monetary Policy Rate by 50 basis points to 26.50% — its second cut since September 2025, when it began easing after holding rates historically high. The logic is sensible: inflation has fallen 10 months in a row, from a 34.19% peak to 15.10% today. Tight rates kept inflation down; now the economy needs cheaper credit to grow faster. Cut rates, stimulate credit-sensitive sectors — manufacturing, trade, real estate — which together account for 40% of GDP.

The tension: this cut happened the same week Brent crossed $91. Higher oil means higher petrol. Higher petrol means higher transport costs. Higher transport costs mean higher food prices. Higher food prices mean inflation potentially reversing. If the February 2026 inflation print — due mid-March — shows a spike, the CBN will have cut rates into a rising inflation environment. That's the kind of policy mistake that has consequences. Governor Cardoso is betting the disinflationary trend holds. The market is watching.

🧂  ADD SALT: The CBN is driving forward while watching the rearview mirror. The rear view (10 months of falling inflation) looks great. The windshield (fuel at ₦975 and climbing) is murkier. We'll know in three weeks which mirror matters.

💵  NAIRA WAHALA

Your currency, your anxiety — explained, not amplified

Madam Market's cousin — the Naira — had a mixed week. She opened Monday at ₦1,360 to the dollar riding strong reserves and a tightening parallel market spread. By Friday March 6th, she had softened to ₦1,385 — a 1.9% week-on-week decline. To the man in the Camry, that's academic. To the importer who needs dollars to pay for a shipment, that ₦25 per dollar matters very much.

WHY THE NAIRA HAS HELD UP BETTER THAN EXPECTED

Four pillars deserve credit — and the CBN deserves to take a bow on each one. First: gross FX reserves at $50.45 billion — 9.68 months of import cover, the highest since 2013. This is ammunition. The CBN can sell dollars into the market to defend the rate and has done so with discipline. Second: the parallel market spread is now 1.5–2.0%. One year ago it was 30–40%. The 'black market' in Lagos and Abuja is now essentially a premium BDC — a convenience fee, not a crisis indicator. Third: Dangote Refinery supplying 40.1 million litres daily reduces the forex Nigeria needs for refined product imports — structurally supporting the Naira. Fourth: inflation falling for 10 months improves real returns on naira-denominated instruments, attracting portfolio inflows.

THE RISK: WHAT COULD BREAK THE STREAK

Two scenarios. Scenario A: the Iran war triggers a global recession — demand for oil collapses, Nigeria's FX inflows shrink, and the reserves stop growing. Scenario B: oil stays high, but Nigeria can't lift more production. Currently at 1.46 million bpd vs a budget target of 2.06 million bpd — a 29% shortfall. We get better per-unit prices but on fewer units. The windfall arrives in a smaller envelope. The OPL 245 deal addresses this long-term. The short-term production gap remains.

🏃  RUN: Watch the parallel market spread daily. If it moves above 5%, that is the first warning signal. At 1.5–2.0% today, we are in healthy territory — but the fuel price surge is a new pressure variable.

🏭  SECTOR SPOTLIGHT: OIL & GAS

The full anatomy of the most important energy week in two years

Uncle Dangote had his most public week since the refinery launched. The Strait of Hormuz had its most consequential week since the Iran-Iraq War. Nigeria sat at the intersection of both — simultaneously collecting the windfall and paying the bill. Let's dissect it.

A CIVILIAN'S GUIDE TO WHAT HAPPENED IN THE MIDDLE EAST

OPERATION EPIC FURY — THE WEEK THAT MOVED MARKETS

Saturday, Feb 28: The US and Israel launch joint strikes on Iran — Operation Epic Fury (US) and Roaring Lion (Israel). Ayatollah Khamenei is killed. Nuclear infrastructure and military command centres are targeted. The world collectively puts down its coffee.

Sunday, March 1: Iran retaliates. Hezbollah opens a northern front from Lebanon. Iran's Revolutionary Guard announces the Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of the world's seaborne crude flows daily — is closed to 'hostile' vessels. Of 24 tankers that normally transit daily, only 4 attempt passage. QatarEnergy halts LNG production after Iranian attacks on its facilities. Saudi Aramco's Ras Tanura refinery catches fire from drone debris.

Monday, March 2: Brent jumps +9% in a single session — the biggest single-day move since COVID. Trump promises insurance guarantees for tankers. Dangote Refinery suspends petrol loading at midnight, then announces a ₦100 gantry price hike at dawn.

Wednesday, March 4: Goldman Sachs raises Q2 Brent forecast by $10. UBS raises full-year 2026 estimate. JPMorgan puts $120/barrel on the table if the Hormuz closure drags past 6 weeks. Qatar Energy Minister Saad al-Kaabi warns prices could hit $150/barrel, enough to 'bring down the economies of the world.' In Alaba Market, a petrol marketer is nodding.

Friday, March 6: Brent closes at $91.23. Weekly gain: +26%. Diesel depot prices in Lagos hit ₦1,200/litre. Pump petrol: ₦975/litre. Cooking gas: ₦1,400/kg. The week is over. The prices are not.

"A third of the world's seaborne crude exits through the Strait of Hormuz. Three-quarters of it goes to China, India, Japan, and South Korea. When the Strait sneezes, Asia catches a cold and Nigeria cashes a cheque — or a bill. This week, both the cheque and the bill arrived on the same day."

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR NIGERIA: THE FULL LEDGER

THE WINDFALL SIDE (The Budget Director's Chart)

Nigeria's 2026 budget was benchmarked at $64.85 per barrel. At $91, every barrel produced generates roughly $26 above benchmark — straight into the federation account, to be shared between FG, states, and local governments. With the federal government staring at a ₦12.14 trillion budget deficit, this windfall is not decorative. It's structural relief. At 1.46 million bpd actual production, Nigeria earns approximately $130 million extra per day compared to budget assumptions. That's real money.

THE BILL SIDE (The Man at Berger's Receipt)

Nigeria's actual production is 1.46 million bpd — 29% below the 2.06 million bpd budget target. Higher price per barrel helps, but lower volume limits the gains — like getting a 40% tip on a smaller restaurant bill. Furthermore, ₦8.36 trillion of oil revenues are pre-committed to servicing crude-backed loans before anything enters the federation account. The windfall is real. But it passes through a very narrow pipe before it arrives.

THE UNCLE DANGOTE SITUATION: A NATION DIVIDED

On the night of March 1st/2nd, the Dangote Petroleum Refinery suspended petrol loading. When trucks returned, the price was ₦874 at the ex-depot gantry — up from ₦774. Within 24 hours, NNPC Retail pump prices hit ₦975/litre. Uncle Dangote issued a 6-paragraph press statement. The nation responded as Nigerians do: with a mix of economic analysis, WhatsApp arguments, and Twitter threads at 2am.

🔴  UNCLE DANGOTE'S CRITICS SAY

🟢  THE REFINERY'S STATEMENT SAYS

You get crude from NNPC in Naira. Why pass full global prices to consumers?

These products were refined before the price spike — you're charging replacement cost for already-made petrol.

Imported fuel is currently ₦64/litre CHEAPER than your petrol. Explain.

You have a near-monopoly and a government naira-for-crude deal. With great refinery comes great responsibility.

NNPC provides 5 of our 18 required monthly crude cargoes in Naira. The other 13 we buy at full dollar international prices.

Nigerian crude costs $3–6 more than Brent benchmark per barrel. After $3.50/barrel freight, crude arrives at $88–91/barrel in our tanks.

We absorbed 20% of the cost increase ourselves — the full pass-through would have been worse.

We are prioritising the domestic market over higher-margin exports specifically to protect Nigerian supply.

Who is right? Both, frustratingly. The structural problem isn't Uncle Dangote's character — it's that Nigeria built one large private refinery, gave it a market, and didn't build a consumer protection mechanism alongside it. The Dangote Refinery is a business, not a public utility. Until a second and third refinery comes online at competitive scale, this is what a monopoly market looks like: efficient, expensive, and unapologetically so.

🧂  ADD SALT: The real conversation Nigeria needs to have isn't 'did Dangote do the right thing?' It's 'why, in 2026, does Nigeria have exactly one functional refinery for 220 million people?' That answer isn't about one man. It's about 40 years of policy failure — and Dangote's refinery, for all its pricing drama, is still the only reason we're not fully import-dependent during a global supply crisis.

THE FORGOTTEN CRISIS: COOKING GAS AT ₦1,400/KG

While petrol grabbed every headline, cooking gas (LPG) quietly surged 40% to ₦1,400 per kilogram this week. LPG is an internationally traded commodity and Nigeria's price is directly linked to global markets. For the millions of Nigerians — particularly in urban centres — who switched from kerosene to gas over the past decade as part of the government's 'clean cooking' push: congratulations on your upgrade. It now costs more than the old fuel you replaced.

This is not a minor inconvenience. This is kitchen-table inflation — the kind that changes what families eat, how often they cook hot meals, and whether small food businesses can afford to operate. The economic cost is distributed quietly across millions of households that will not issue a press release about it.

THE POWER GRID: THE CRISIS NOBODY TALKS ABOUT IN POLITE COMPANY

On Thursday March 5, Nigeria's national grid dropped to 3,940 MW. By Friday March 6 it had fallen to 2,898 MW — an 11% single-day decline. Thermal power plants are receiving 41% of the gas they need: 652 MMSCF per day vs a requirement of 1,588 MMSCF. Let that ratio sink in. The factories this country needs to manufacture its way into the middle-income tier are running on generators at 3–5x the cost of grid power. The manufacturers pass those costs to consumers. Consumers can't buy as much. The economy underperforms its potential. This is the invisible tax that no newspaper puts on the front page but that every factory, bakery, cold room, and data centre in Nigeria pays — every single day.

🫴  We are an oil-producing country where fuel is expensive, a gas-producing country where cooking gas cost too much, and an electricity-generating country where the light no dey. This is not irony. This is policy. And policy can be changed. Slowly. But it can.

💡  WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOUR MONEY:  For investors: energy sector plays (Seplat, Total Nigeria, Conoil) haven't fully priced in $91 Brent. For business owners: if your generator fuel cost just jumped 12%, reprice before your competition reprices without you. For everyone: solar + inverter is no longer a luxury — it's a hedge.

🌍  NAIJA & THE WORLD

Global macro that always finds its way back to your street

THREE RISKS NIGERIA ISN'T TALKING ABOUT LOUDLY ENOUGH

1. Domestic religious and security tension. The Islamic Movement of Nigeria (IMN) held protests across six northern states following the killing of Ayatollah Khamenei. This is not a fringe event. Iran has historically maintained relationships with certain West African groups, and Nigeria's CBN and RIMSON have explicitly flagged that the conflict could 'influence diplomatic relations and regional stability.' The Nigerian government handled this quietly. The quietness is appropriate. The risk is real.

2. Air travel disruption. Hundreds of Nigerian passengers were stranded as Gulf airspaces closed. Nigeria is one of Africa's largest markets for Gulf travel — pilgrims, traders, professionals. The Hajj route disruption is being managed diplomatically, but the economic cost of weeks of disrupted air connectivity accumulates.

3. Global recession risk — the oil spike that kills demand. Nigeria wants high oil prices. But oil above $100/barrel for sustained periods historically triggers recessions in oil-importing economies — particularly in Asia, which buys most of our crude. Lower Chinese and Indian demand = lower oil prices = the windfall reverses. The sweet spot for Nigeria is $75–$90: high enough to fund the budget, low enough not to destroy the buyers. At $91 we are at the edge of that range.

OPEC+ PLOT TWIST: ADDING BARRELS INTO A CLOSED STRAIT

Eight OPEC+ members — Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, Oman — agreed to increase production by 206,000 bpd in April, higher than analyst expectations. The peculiarity: they're adding supply while the Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed. Extra barrels can't easily leave the Gulf. This reads less like an economic decision and more like a diplomatic signal to the US: 'We don't want $150 oil either. We remember 2008.' The Saudis in particular have enough history with demand destruction to know exactly where the price becomes self-defeating.

🫴  The global economy dey act confused this week. Oil at $91, war dey, but US stock market still dey up, Bitcoin crossed $73,000, and somewhere, a crypto bro is explaining to his mother why her cooking gas and his portfolio are both rising. Different markets, same world.

💡  WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOUR MONEY:  Nigeria's macro position — high reserves, falling inflation, strong market — gives us a buffer that most of our neighbours don't have. That buffer is not infinite. Use it wisely: this is not the week to be taking on speculative naira liabilities.

THE CENTRAL PARADOX OF THE WEEK

Nigeria is an oil-exporting nation that suffers when oil prices rise. Read that again. Slowly. We produce the commodity. We don't refine enough of it. We get the export revenue in dollars, but we pay the retail pain in naira. The theoretical windfall of $91 oil is real: extra FAAC allocations, deficit reduction, reserve accumulation. The practical experience of $91 oil is ₦975/litre petrol, ₦1,400/kg cooking gas, ₦1,200/litre diesel, and a national grid generating less power than some African cities. These are not contradictions. They are the consequence of building an oil economy without building the downstream infrastructure to translate export wealth into consumer welfare.

Every country that has solved this problem — Norway, UAE, Saudi Arabia — did it the same way: domestic refining capacity at scale, a sovereign wealth fund that captures windfall revenue and protects against price crashes, and a deliberate policy of insulating citizens from commodity volatility. Nigeria is in the process of building all three. The Dangote Refinery is Act One. The Tinubu Renewed Hope Infrastructure Fund is attempting Act Two. Act Three — a functioning sovereign wealth fund that isn't perpetually raided — remains aspirational.

"Nigeria is the only country in the world that can simultaneously be winning on oil prices and losing on fuel prices. We are the irony capital of the global energy market. But we're fixing it — chapter by painful chapter."

The OPL 245 deal is the right move. The timeline is 3–5 years. The windfall is now. That mismatch is Nigeria's perpetual condition: the right structures being built on the right timeline, while the present remains expensive.
