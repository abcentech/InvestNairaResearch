THE WEEK IN THREE SENTENCES

The CBN closed the most consequential chapter in Nigerian banking since 2004 — 33 banks raised ₦4.65 trillion in new capital, proving that when a Nigerian regulator actually holds a deadline, the market responds — and then immediately announced the next exam: Risk-Based Capital stress tests, due April 30.

Iran and the US agreed to a "two-week ceasefire" over the Strait of Hormuz, Brent crude collapsed $28 in five sessions — Nigeria's best oil windfall scenario vanished like ₦5,000 change at a Lagos petrol station — and every financial model built on $100+ oil now needs a rewrite.

Lagos pump prices rose to ₦1,332 the same week — the Trade Union Congress called it a national emergency, danfo drivers said conductors were begging passengers to board, and workers confirmed they spend 80% of their income on transport, which means the other 20% is the economy we keep calling "resilient."

₦4.65TN RAISED. NOW SURVIVE THE TEST.

On April 2, CBN Governor Olayemi Cardoso announced the end of the two-year recapitalisation programme. Thirty-three banks cleared. ₦4.65 trillion raised. Seventy-two percent of it sourced domestically — meaning Nigerian pension funds, retail investors, and institutions looked at the numbers and decided to stay. That is the most important sentence in this section. Not the ₦4.65 trillion. The fact that most of it was local. In a country where money has been fleeing the system for three years, investors choosing to put ₦3.4 trillion of it back in is not a number. It is a verdict.

Cardoso inherited a sector where "forbearance" had become a permanent item in bank balance sheet footnotes — which is the financial equivalent of your doctor noting "patient refuses to check" in your file and calling it healthcare. He set a deadline in March 2024. He held it in March 2026. He then immediately raised the bar. The April 30 Risk-Based Capital stress test simulates the worst case: oil at $60, Naira at ₦2,000, NPLs hitting 15% simultaneously. Every bank must submit a Board-approved report proving their capital can absorb that. Because Cardoso knows, and every analyst knows, that capital quantity is easy to fake on paper. Capital quality is what the crisis finds out.

Three banks — Union, Polaris, Keystone — did not cross the finish line under their own power. The CBN's statement on them reads: "ongoing regulatory and judicial processes." That is the diplomatic version. The operational version: no expansion, no large new loans, supervised exits for some. Depositors are safe. That last sentence is both true and the specific sentence you issue when you need everyone to stay calm. Both things can be true at the same time in Nigeria. We have extensive experience with this.

THE $28 THAT APPEARED, BOWED, AND DEPARTED.

On Wednesday, Trump announced a "double-sided ceasefire" with Iran. Iran agreed to reopen the Strait of Hormuz for two weeks — "coordinated with Iran's Armed Forces" — which is a ceasefire the way a toll gate is a gift. The markets, unbothered by the fine print, priced $28 off the barrel in a single session. It was the largest single-week oil price drop since March 2020, when COVID announced itself to the world by cancelling everything.

For Nigeria, the arithmetic still works. The 2026 budget was benchmarked at $64.85 per barrel. The TUC — bless them — pointed this out when they demanded the government redirect the windfall revenue (roughly $35 above benchmark at recent prices) to subsidise petrol prices. Oga Macro's response has been the sound of a spreadsheet staying closed. At $90, Nigeria is still earning above budget. At $80, the conversation changes. At $70 — J.P. Morgan's full-year 2026 forecast — Ojulari needs a new slide deck for London.

The harder truth is that the $40 of oil upside that existed at $118 was borrowed from a war. A war in which Nigeria had no soldiers, no vote, no seat at the table, and no say in the ceasefire. The money was there. Then it wasn't. The pump price, which absorbed none of the fall, had already absorbed the rise — twice. This is what energy economists call "asymmetric pass-through." What Emeka in Surulere calls it is not printable in a family newsletter.
