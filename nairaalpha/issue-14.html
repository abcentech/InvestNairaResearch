THE WEEK OIL WENT ON A ROUNDABOUT AND YOUR PUMP PRICE STAYED HOME.

On Monday morning, Brent was $116.55 — a ten-week-high sustained by the Hormuz closure, IEA supply-shock warnings, and a fundamental absence of any clarity on when tankers might flow again. Nigeria was earning $41 above its $75 budget benchmark on every barrel. Oga Macro's spreadsheet had never looked more optimistic. Dangote raised its gantry price to ₦1,350 per litre. The template was sent on Tuesday. Filling stations across Lagos received the new pricing list and updated their boards. Everything was proceeding with the mechanical efficiency that Nigerian downstream pricing exhibits on the upside — which is to say, very quickly.

Then on Wednesday at approximately 2pm, Trump posted: "Iran is seriously considering our proposal. This could end very soon." Brent fell $10.03 in a single session. Traders who had been pricing 10 weeks of Hormuz disruption suddenly had to price a scenario where the Strait reopened. The drop was the fastest single-session move since the conflict began. By close: $106.52. By Thursday morning, Iran's foreign ministry had confirmed it had received no such proposal. Pakistan, the designated intermediary, said talks were ongoing. Fresh US-Iran naval clashes occurred in the Gulf. Brent edged back to $100. By Thursday it was $101.65. Na so the market take behave when nobody know anything.

Here is the specific detail that Naira Alpha will not allow to pass without comment: Brent fell $15 between Monday and Thursday. Dangote's gantry price, set on Tuesday at ₦1,350, did not change. On Wednesday when oil crashed, a senior Dangote official told Naija247 the refinery was "monitoring market conditions." On Thursday, the price was confirmed unchanged — still ₦1,350. The IEA notes that even if the Hormuz opens, production recovery from damaged GCC infrastructure will take months. The refinery is technically correct that costs remain elevated. The Nigerian consumer, however, is watching a $10 single-day oil crash produce zero change at the pump and has questions.

The broader picture for Nigeria is complex. Higher Brent means more export revenue above budget — the Naira benefits from that. Lower Brent means cheaper input costs for the refinery — the consumer should benefit from that. In practice, the revenue goes into the budget and the savings don't reach the pump in any predictable timeframe. This is not new information. It is just being illustrated, again, this week, with a $10 data point and a ₦0 pump price change.

THE NAIRA'S QUIET REVOLUTION. THE ONE NOBODY IS CELEBRATING LOUDLY ENOUGH.

Nairametrics ran a piece this week titled "How a Stable Naira is Quietly Fixing Nigeria." The timing is notable because it is true and underreported. The official exchange rate is ₦1,359–₦1,370 this week — down from ₦1,600+ in early 2025, and nearing the ₦1,350 level that analysts have called the symbolic "next milestone." Daily volatility has dropped from above 4% in 2024 to around 0.5%. The parallel market spread, which was ₦200+ at the peak of the FX crisis, has compressed back toward ₦30–₦40 this week after briefly hitting ₦2. E don stable, small small.

The structural reason for this, as Nairametrics correctly identifies, is the Dangote Refinery. Before the refinery, Nigeria spent approximately $7–8 billion per year importing refined petroleum products. Every dollar used to buy petrol from Rotterdam was a dollar that had to leave the formal FX system, create parallel market pressure, and weaken the Naira. The refinery has fundamentally altered this dynamic: Nigeria now refines locally, pays for crude in naira (under the naira-for-crude arrangement), and does not need to find dollars for refined product imports at the same scale. The result is structural reduction in FX demand, which supports the Naira regardless of what happens with oil prices internationally.

The irony — and there is always an irony with Nigeria — is that the same refinery whose existence is stabilising the Naira is also setting gantry prices that feed inflation, which feeds transport costs, which feeds the same inflation the stable Naira is supposed to be helping to reduce. The Naira is being fixed by the refinery. The cost of living is being pressured by the refinery. The refinery is, simultaneously, Nigeria's greatest structural achievement of the decade and your most reliable source of bad news on Thursday mornings. Na correct Nigerian institution be that.
