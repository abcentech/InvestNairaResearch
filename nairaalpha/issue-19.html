THE SEQUEL — NOW THE POLITICIANS OPEN THEIR WALLETS

₦874 billion is the government’s entrance fee. The real party hasn’t started yet.

The new Electoral Act 2026 permits a presidential candidate to legally spend ₦10 billion on campaign. Governorship: ₦3 billion. Senate: ₦500 million. House: ₦250 million. Now multiply by 36 governors, 109 senators, 360 House members, 1,491 state assembly seats. You now have a number that requires its own zip code. Add INEC’s ₦874 billion on top. And that is still only the declared money.

🌶🌶🌶 THE PEPPER: WHAT ₦10 BILLION DOES NOT INCLUDE

The ₦10B cap is a legal fiction. A creative writing exercise by legislators who write the rules they plan to break. The real money — the kind that floods markets and pressures every price you pay — includes things INEC cannot audit and will not investigate:

🤷State governors “mobilising support” — with state budgets, state vehicles, state helicopters, and state police escorts. None of it counts as campaign spend. All of it costs you, the taxpayer.
💵“Thank you” envelopes at rallies — ₦5,000 per market woman, ₦10,000 per okada riders’ association chairman, ₦50,000 for the youth group coordinator. Cash. No receipts. Hot naira, straight into the street economy.
🎊“Community development” donations — transformer here, borehole there, church roof, mosque generator, primary school painting. They call it constituency work. INEC calls it nothing, because INEC never sees it.
👔The “facilitators” — the gentlemen in agbada at the hotel lobby with the briefcases. We have all heard about them. INEC has not met them and has no plans to do so.
⚡Every single naira of this lands in the real economy. It bids the parallel rate up. It pressures food prices. It is someone’s problem — and that someone is holding this newsletter right now.

Here is the delicious irony nobody discusses at the Abuja cocktail parties: the politicians about to flood the economy with hot campaign naira are the same people who need Cardoso’s monetary discipline to survive. They need a stable naira so the dollar doesn’t run before they finish campaigning. They are, in one sentence, the single biggest threat to the very ground beneath their own campaign podiums. Lagos irony. Served cold. With a side of jollof. 🍎

ACT II — THE STREET

Leave the spreadsheet. Step outside. The real economy doesn’t care what NAFEM says.

Thirty-four degrees. Balogun Market. Your shirt surrendered two hours ago. Alhaji is on his plastic chair under a yellow umbrella that has seen better decades, counting dollar bills with the calm of a man who knows he holds something everyone needs — and nobody wants to admit they need.

“Alhaji, NAFEM dey ₦1,361 o. Make we do ₦1,370?”

He doesn’t look up. He fans himself. With the dollar bills. “My friend. NAFEM na television. Street na ₦1,400. School fees season. Summer holiday. Everybody want dollar. You buy at ₦1,400 or you call Cardoso direct. No middle man for CBN number.”

That ₦39 gap between what the CBN window says and what Alhaji actually charges is not a rounding error. It is the distance between a press release and your actual life. Between the official aquarium[1] and the open ocean where the sharks are always, always hungry.

ACT III — THE INVISIBLE HAND AND ITS FRAGILITY

Cardoso is winning. Right now. Here is exactly why that might not last — and what to do about your money before it changes.

Amid all of this, something unusual is happening at the macro level. Foreign reserves: $49.88 billion. Official NAFEM rate: strengthened to ₦1,361. S&P credit: B — Nigeria’s best in 14 years. Your stockbroker is suffering, but the CBN’s balance sheet is thriving. How?

A mechanism both beautiful and ruthless: the carry trade. Foreign investors bring dollars, convert to naira at NAFEM, buy 26.5% government T-bills, collect the yield, then leave. While they are here, the naira is defended. Reserves grow. Cardoso looks calm. The carry trade is doing the work that three decades of oil revenue couldn’t.

The problem: carry trade money wears running shoes. It comes with a return ticket already booked. The moment another market offers better yield — or Nigeria’s political risk spikes — it walks. Fast. And when it walks, it takes the exchange rate with it. Every. Single. Time.

Which is why your stockbroker is crying into their terminal. The NGX All-Share dropped 3.2% to 242,258.49 points. Not because Nigeria’s companies got worse overnight. Because capital is perfectly rational: a risk-free government bond at 26.5% makes your bank stock’s volatile 12% dividend look like charity. Money flows where it is treated best. Today, that is Cardoso’s T-bill window. Tomorrow depends on what happens in Abuja.

“The politicians flooding the economy with hot campaign naira are standing on Cardoso’s stability to deliver their speeches. They are the single biggest threat to the very ground beneath their feet. Classic Nigeria. Served cold. With jollof.”

The uncomfortable conclusion: Cardoso’s carry trade works until politics makes it not work. The date politics gets loud is not a mystery — it is budgeted, it is on a calendar, and INEC has already been paid ₦874 billion to prepare for it. The only open question is whether your portfolio is positioned before the noise arrives — or scrambling to reposition after Alhaji’s umbrella starts doing overtime.
💡 WHAT SMART MONEY IS DOING RIGHT NOW
Three calls. Not speculation. Grounded in everything you just read. Treat it like advice from a friend who runs a macro desk.

1. Lock your dollar obligations for Q3/Q4 today.

NAFEM is ₦1,361 today. The 2027 political liquidity surge is not theoretical — it is budgeted, scheduled, and funded by both the government and the candidates. School fees, import orders, FX-denominated rent: price them now. ₦1,400 parallel is uncomfortable. ₦1,600 in January 2027 is worse. Alhaji already knows this. His umbrella is already ordering an upgrade.

2. Ride the T-bill window — but set an alarm for Q3 2026.

26.5% risk-free is extraordinary. It is also a crisis-era rate designed to attract the carry trade. It is not a pension strategy. When Cardoso cuts — or when political pressure forces his hand — yield drops fast and carry-trade money runs. Ride it hard through Q3 2026. Then reassess. This is a sprint with a known finish line.

3. Rotate equities into defensive shelter now.

If you hold high-beta bank stocks bleeding into the T-bill rotation, consider rotating into telcos and consumer staples. MTN, Airtel Africa, Dangote Cement pass through cost in an inflationary environment better than retail banks when rates are this punishing. Diversify or bleed. Those are the two options currently on the menu.
