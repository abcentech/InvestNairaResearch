₦7Tn.
SITTING IDLE AT THE CBN.
That is how much Nigerian bank cash was parked at the CBN's Standing Deposit Facility this week. Idle. Earning a modest overnight rate. Financing nothing. Not a road, not a factory, not your cousin's catering startup. Just sitting there, blinking.
EDITOR'S NOTE

Wednesday, March 11. 11:43am. A trading desk at a Lagos Island asset management firm. A portfolio manager in a powder-blue kaftan stares at a Brent crude ticker that just flashed $101.20 a barrel — triple digits — for the first time since the Iran war began. He does not celebrate. He knows that somewhere between that number and his morning commute, there is a filling station charging ₦1,200 a litre. He knows that across town, the Senate just adjourned till March 31 without passing the 2026 budget. He turns to his colleague and says the one thing that explains everything: "The numbers are amazing. The execution is a journey."

Brent crude crossed $101 intraday on Wednesday — the first triple-digit print since the war began — before settling at $99.80 by Friday.

Nigeria's banks completed the most significant recapitalisation in twenty years, with 31 of 33 banks meeting the CBN's new capital requirements — and are now sitting on ₦7 trillion in excess liquidity they don't know how to deploy.

And the 2026 federal budget still has not passed, the Senate adjourned to March 31, leaving Nigeria staring at a potential ₦30 trillion oil windfall with no legal framework yet to receive it.

🏛️  THE MAIN STORY

The budget that isn't — and why it's the most expensive silence in Abuja

Nigeria is 103 days into the 2026 fiscal year without a passed budget. The ₦58.18 trillion spending plan — which includes ₦26 trillion in capital expenditure for roads, power, education, and health — has been sitting with the National Assembly since December. Without a passed budget, capital releases are frozen, ministries are rationing, and the multiplier effect of government spending simply isn't running.

Now layer the oil windfall on top: the NESG published a policy brief titled "Boom, Not Gloom" mapping a potential ₦30.2 trillion in extra oil revenue if the Hormuz disruption persists through Q2. That is transformational money. But without a passed budget, the government has no legal framework to save it, deploy it, or protect it. The windfall is coming. The box to put it in is under construction.

Finance Minister Wale Edun explicitly ruled out petrol price controls this week — holding the deregulation line despite ₦1,200/litre and very loud political pressure. That deserves credit. But Edun is making the right macroeconomic argument to a population experiencing the wrong kitchen-table reality.

"The windfall is real. The machinery to receive it doesn't legally exist yet. Nigeria is standing in front of the biggest payday of this decade, and the Senate adjourned."

🏦  SECTOR SPOTLIGHT: BANKING

The recapitalisation endgame — 31 done, 2 to go, ₦7 trillion idle

In March 2024, CBN Governor Cardoso announced banks would need to meet significantly higher minimum capital requirements by March 31, 2026. Two years later, 31 of 33 banks have crossed the finish line. The two remaining are the week's cliffhanger — one may raise capital at the wire, one may be acquired.

When the exercise was announced, Brent was ~$80. Today, as banks cross the finish line, Brent is at $100 and Nigeria is staring at a potential ₦30 trillion windfall. The banks that recapitalised just positioned themselves to intermediate the largest oil revenue cycle Nigeria may see this decade. The timing — largely coincidental — is remarkable.

THE ₦7 TRILLION ELEPHANT

Nigerian banks collectively placed ₦7 trillion in excess cash at the CBN's Standing Deposit Facility — the window where banks park money they don't know what to do with. That's approximately 12% of Nigeria's entire annual GDP sitting idle, earning a modest overnight rate rather than being deployed through loans. Banks are liquid. Banks are also nervous. The CBN's rate cut to 26.5% was supposed to nudge this cash into the economy. Watch whether the SDF balance falls below ₦5 trillion by end of Q2 — that's the real leading indicator of whether Nigeria's recovery has reached the real economy.

📈  THE FLOOR

NGX +47.8% YTD. Madam Market is not playing.

The NGX is up 47.8% year-to-date. The S&P 500 is up roughly 3% in the same period. Madam Market is not walking — she is sprinting in a freshly recapitalised banking sector. NGX Group itself declared ₦15.6bn pre-tax profit for 2025, a ₦3.00/share dividend, a 1-for-3 bonus share, and is up approximately 319% year-on-year. When the exchange is one of the best performers on the exchange — that is either beautiful irony or a genuine signal the entire ecosystem is strengthening.

Also: Seplat Energy announced 17 new wells and $440 million in capex for 2026 — the most aggressive indigenous upstream program in years, timed perfectly into $100 oil. The market may not have fully priced this in yet.

⚖️  THE VERDICT

VILLAIN OF THE WEEK DOWNGRADE

The Senate. Not because they are incompetent — but because the cost of this adjournment is specific and measurable. Every week without a passed budget is ~₦500bn in capital expenditure delayed. At a 1.4x multiplier, that's ₦700bn in economic activity not generated per week. The Senators go home. The contractors wait. The roads don't get built. The ₦30 trillion windfall arrives into an empty room.

HERO OF THE WEEK UPGRADE

CBN Governor Olayemi Cardoso. The bank recapitalisation exercise he designed, pressured, and saw through to 31/33 completion is arguably the most consequential institutional achievement in Nigerian banking since Soludo's 2005 consolidation. He didn't announce it loudly. He didn't need to. The balance sheets speak for themselves.

🔮  THE ALPHA

Our take. Our call. Checkable next Saturday.

Last week we had difficult problems. This week Nigeria has good problems: a potential ₦30 trillion windfall with no budget framework to receive it; banks flush with ₦7 trillion in liquidity with nowhere productive to deploy it; oil at $100 with no mechanism to convert export earnings into domestic relief on a timeline shorter than 60 days. Good problems are still problems. But they are the kind that, if solved, compound rather than subtract.
